Iron Maiden · Coliseum Building · Ottawa, ON · June 14, 1992

The Longest Song in the World

The music and the room. The band and the moment.

The Venue
The Coliseum · Lansdowne Park
Bank Street, Ottawa · Built 1903 · Demolished 2012

The Coliseum at Lansdowne Park, Ottawa, 1947 · Photo: rdb466 / Flickr · CC BY 2.0

The Coliseum Building at Lansdowne Park was not the kind of room Iron Maiden was used to playing.

In their prime — 1984, 1985, the World Slavery Tour — Maiden were filling 15,000-seat arenas across North America without breaking a sweat. The Montreal Forum. Maple Leaf Gardens. The Spectrum in Philadelphia. They were one of the biggest metal bands on the planet, and the rooms reflected it. By June 1992, the Ottawa stop of the Fear of the Dark tour landed them in a repurposed exhibition hall on Bank Street, a building that had been hosting livestock shows and trade fairs since 1903. Capacity somewhere around 5,000. And from the accounts of people who were there, it wasn't full.

That tells you something important about where Iron Maiden stood in the summer of 1992. And it makes what happened that night — one of the earliest North American performances of "Fear of the Dark," a song that had been on an album for barely a month — stranger and more significant than it might otherwise seem.

The decline had been building for years, and it had a name: Nevermind.

More precisely, it had several names — Nevermind, Ten, Badmotorfinger, three albums that arrived within a two-month window in the autumn of 1991 and rewrote the rules of what rock music was supposed to sound like and look like and mean. Suddenly the elaborate theatrics of 1980s metal — the pyro, the mascots, the fantasy mythology, the codpieces — looked not just dated but slightly embarrassing. The kids who had grown up on Maiden in 1984 were now buying Soundgarden records and wondering why they'd ever cared about Eddie.

The attendance figures don't lie. Maiden were playing smaller venues and fewer dates than ever on their North American run, and if Dickinson and Harris had hoped that Fear of the Dark would rejuvenate their popularity Stateside, they were faced with utter failure. The previous tour, supporting No Prayer for the Dying in 1990-91, had already seen a significant drop. The Fear of the Dark tour dropped further. Venues that had once been too small were now too big.

The Coliseum Building in Ottawa — an old exhibition hall, not an arena, not a stadium — was exactly the right size for where Maiden were in North America in 1992. That is both a fact and an indictment.

The band itself was fraying at the edges, though few outside the inner circle knew it yet.

Adrian Smith — co-writer of some of Maiden's most beloved songs, the guitarist whose melodic sensibility had balanced Steve Harris's more bombastic instincts for nearly a decade — had already left, replaced by Janick Gers before No Prayer for the Dying was even recorded. The loss was felt more in the texture of the music than in any single song, but it was real. Maiden without Smith was a slightly different band, and not entirely a better one.

And then there was Dickinson.

Publicly, he talked up Fear of the Dark as a modern and groundbreaking album for a new age. Privately, he was agonizing. Two thirds of the way through the tour, by his own later admission, he was "utterly depressed, totally miserable." "I think I've been creatively sleepwalking for the last five years," he would later say. "The rest of the band and all the fans love being locked in the straight, narrow direction that is Maiden, and there's nothing wrong with that. But I kept trying to deviate from the rut, saying, 'Look what's up here guys!' I just ended up drained. I realised I was trying to drag this huge thing somewhere it didn't want to go."

He hadn't told anyone yet. He wouldn't announce his departure until early 1993. But the decision was forming somewhere in the back of his mind as the tour bus rolled through North America in the summer of 1992, stopping at mid-sized venues in cities that had once been easy sells. He was performing. He was delivering. But part of him was already elsewhere.

The Coliseum Building on Bank Street had been a fixture of Ottawa life for nearly ninety years by the time Maiden arrived. Built in 1903 as a livestock exhibition hall for the Central Canada Exhibition, it had hosted political conventions, trade shows, the Ottawa 67's ticket office, and by 1992, indoor soccer in the winters. It was a building that had outlasted its original purpose and kept finding new ones — solid, functional, unremarkable from the outside, cavernous within.

It was not a purpose-built rock venue. The sound bounced off industrial walls in ways that an arena's designed acoustics would have absorbed. The lighting rigs were brought in. The floor was flat, which meant the kids at the back were craning to see over the kids at the front. It was the kind of room where a band plays because the tour is routing through Ottawa and this is what's available, not because this is where you'd choose to make a statement.

And yet.

Iron Maiden had released Fear of the Dark on May 11, 1992 — just over a month before the Ottawa show. The album had entered the UK chart at number one, their third to do so. In North America the reception was cooler, the sales more modest. But the album contained something that nobody quite knew yet was going to outlast everything around it.

The title track — nine and a half minutes, building from an acoustic introduction through escalating guitar figures to a full-band crescendo — was unlike anything in the Maiden catalogue. It wasn't a warrior's anthem or a history lesson or a mythology exercise. It was genuinely, vulnerably human: a song about being afraid of the dark, about that specific childhood terror that never entirely leaves you, about the feeling of something watching from the shadows that reason cannot dispel. Harris wrote it partly about his own experience driving home late at night on empty country roads and feeling, despite himself, afraid. That honesty is audible in every bar.

The song's structure rewards patience in a way few metal songs do. The acoustic opening is almost delicate. The tension builds slowly, deliberately. And when the full band finally arrives — when the crowd, if they know it at all, can feel the release coming — the effect is unlike almost anything else in the genre. It became, over subsequent tours, the song that ends every Iron Maiden show. The song that 80,000 people sing back to the stage at Donington. The anthem that somehow got more powerful the further it got from its origins.

But in Ottawa on June 14, 1992, nobody knew any of that yet.

The album had been out for a month. The song had barely been on radio. The tour had opened in New York six days earlier, with a warm-up date in Norwich before that under a pseudonym. Quebec City had heard it the night before. Ottawa was among the first handfuls of North American crowds to receive it — a room in June 1992 where the song arrived still fresh, before it had accumulated any weight of history, before anyone knew what it was going to become.

The crowd at the Coliseum Building — smaller than it should have been, in a room not quite built for this — were among the very first to hear it live. They just didn't know what they were witnessing.

Dickinson left Maiden the following year. The farewell tour of 1993 was awkward and occasionally poisonous — Steve Harris later said "I really wanted to kill him," and accounts of Dickinson's attitude on stage ranged from inspired to disengaged depending on who was watching and when. The era ended on August 28, 1993 at Pinewood Studios, filmed by the BBC, with a magician theatrically decapitating Dickinson on stage. The symbolism was not subtle.

And then something unexpected happened.

Dickinson came back in 1999, bringing Adrian Smith with him. The reunion should have been awkward — six years of estrangement, two failed albums with Blaze Bayley, a band that had spent the late 1990s playing venues even smaller than the Ottawa Coliseum. Instead it became one of the great second acts in rock history. The band that emerged from that reunion was harder, more ambitious, and in many ways more successful than the version that had played Ottawa in 1992. They sold out stadiums. They flew their own custom 747, painted to look like Eddie. They headlined Glastonbury. They played Rock in Rio to 250,000 people.

All of that turbulence — the grunge years, the shrinking venues, the Dickinson departure, the Bayley era, the reunion — was still ahead of the crowd at the Coliseum Building on June 14, 1992. What they couldn't know, what nobody could have known, was that the song they heard that night — still barely a week into its live existence — would outlast all of it.

"Fear of the Dark" has been on the setlist of virtually every Iron Maiden tour since 1992. It survived the Dickinson years, the Bayley years, the reunion, and every subsequent era. It is the song that ends the show. The one that 80,000 people sing back at the stage at Download and Donington and Rock in Rio, arms in the air, every word committed to memory. The song that journalists and fans consistently rank among the greatest in the band's catalogue — alongside "The Trooper," "Hallowed Be Thy Name," "Aces High" — songs from the imperial years of the 1980s when Iron Maiden could do no wrong.

It was written during the darkest commercial period of the band's career. It was played live in a half-empty exhibition hall in Ottawa in the first week of its existence, before anyone knew what it would become. It became an anthem that has outlasted almost everything that surrounded its creation — the era, the lineup, the venues, the turbulence.

The seed planted in the difficult soil of 1992 turned out to be the one that grew tallest.

The Coliseum Building was demolished in 2012, part of the Lansdowne Park redevelopment. The room is gone. The building that hosted trade shows and livestock exhibitions and, on one June night in 1992, one of the first performances of one of rock's most enduring anthems — gone.

The stage remembers.
